Instrument Calibration

Calibration procedures, standards, and best practices for industrial instruments and transmitters.

What is Calibration?

Calibration is the process of comparing an instrument's measurement against a known standard or reference to determine accuracy and make necessary adjustments. It ensures instruments provide reliable and traceable measurements.

Calibration Methods

  • Zero & Span Calibration – Two-point adjustment (0% and 100%)
  • Multi-point Calibration – Five or more points for nonlinearity
  • Trim Calibration – Fine-tuning for specific range
  • Sensor Replacement Calibration – New sensor matching
  • In-situ Calibration – Calibration without removal from process

Calibration Standards

  • ISO 9001 – Quality management calibration requirements
  • ISO 17025 – Testing and calibration laboratories
  • ANSI/NCSL Z540 – Calibration laboratories
  • NIST Traceable – National Institute of Standards

Calibration Equipment

  • Pressure Calibrators – Hand pumps, dead-weight testers
  • Temperature Calibrators – Dry-block, liquid baths
  • mA Loop Calibrators – 4-20mA simulation and measurement
  • Multi-function Calibrators – Voltage, current, resistance
  • HART Communicators – Digital communication with smart transmitters

Example: Pressure Transmitter Calibration

# Pressure Transmitter Calibration (0-100 bar, 4-20mA) # Apply pressure values and record output: 0 bar → 4.00 mA (Zero) 25 bar → 8.00 mA 50 bar → 12.00 mA 75 bar → 16.00 mA 100 bar → 20.00 mA (Span) # If 75 bar reads 15.80 mA (error), adjust trim settings.

A calibration report includes before/after readings, uncertainty, and traceability information.
